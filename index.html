The Crave Method

Going to the source —
not the symptom.

Most approaches manage food behavior at the surface. The Crave method addresses the nervous system state that drives the behavior — the layer where change actually sticks.

Why this works

The compulsion lives
below conscious control.

When stress activates the nervous system, the brain does exactly what it's designed to do: it scans for the fastest available relief. For many people, food has become that relief — not by choice, but by repetition. The brain learned. The pattern became automatic.

This is a subcortical process. It completes before the prefrontal cortex — the seat of willpower and conscious decision-making — even registers what's happening. By the time you "decide" to eat, the decision has already been made at a deeper level.

This is why willpower fails. Not because you lack it — but because you're bringing a conscious tool to an unconscious process. The tool was never designed to reach that layer.

The Crave method works directly with the nervous system's own regulation pathways. It doesn't suppress or distract from the compulsion — it addresses the underlying activation that generates it. When the activation resolves, the signal quiets. Sometimes within a single session.

The practice

How a session
actually works.

1
Rate your craving intensity
Before anything else, you check in. Where is the craving on a scale of 0 to 10? This isn't about judgment — it's about creating a before point so you can see what changes.
2
Answer a few short questions
The questionnaire identifies which layer of the protocol you're working with today. Different emotional states and different stages of the process call for different practices. The questions do the routing.
3
Watch your assigned practice
A short guided video — typically 2 minutes — walks you through the nervous system regulation practice matched to your current state. Nothing is required of you except attention and willingness.
4
Rate your craving again
After the practice, you rate again. The difference — however large or small — is real data about your nervous system responding. Over time this data becomes your most powerful evidence that something is genuinely changing.
5
Return to your day
That's it. No homework. No journaling. No behavior contract. Just a 2-minute practice and a data point. The changes happen gradually, between sessions, as the nervous system learns a new baseline.
What to expect

Results that feel different
from anything you've tried.

First session
Most people notice something subtle — a breath that goes a little deeper, a slight drop in the urgency. Some notice nothing at first. Both are fine. The nervous system is responding either way.
First week
The craving may still arise, but its quality begins to shift. A small gap appears between the impulse and the action. That gap is the nervous system learning it has another option.
First month
The gap widens. People describe cravings arising and then fading on their own — not because they resisted, but because the underlying activation had somewhere to go. The compulsion becomes optional.
